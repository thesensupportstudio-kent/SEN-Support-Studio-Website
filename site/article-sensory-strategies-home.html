Five Sensory Strategies to Try at Home

Resources

Practical, no-equipment-needed ideas to start supporting your child's sensory needs today.

← Back to Resources

By Emma Owen, Qualified Teacher & SEN Consultant

Sensory processing differences can make ordinary moments - getting dressed, mealtimes, the walk to school - feel overwhelming for a child, and confusing for the adults trying to help. You don't need special equipment or a diagnosis to start supporting a child's sensory needs at home. Here are five simple places to begin.

1. Build in movement before you need calm

A few minutes of "heavy work" - carrying books, pushing against a wall, star jumps - before a transition (like leaving for school) can help regulate a child's nervous system in advance, rather than trying to calm them down after they've already tipped into overwhelm.

2. Notice the room, not just the child

Fluorescent lighting, background noise, strong smells, or scratchy clothing can all be quietly draining for a sensory-sensitive child. Small changes - a lamp instead of an overhead light, tags cut out of clothes - often make a bigger difference than they sound like they should.

3. Offer a "sensory box"

A small box of textured, calming objects (a stress ball, something soft, something with a satisfying weight) that a child can reach for when they need to self-regulate gives them a tool that's theirs, on their terms.

4. Warn before you switch activities

Transitions are often the hardest part of the day for sensory-sensitive children. A simple countdown or visual timer - "five more minutes, then bath time" - gives their nervous system time to prepare.

5. Watch for the pattern, not just the moment

A meltdown rarely comes from nowhere. Keeping a loose note of what happened just before (loud environment, missed nap, change of plan) often reveals a pattern worth working around, rather than reacting to each incident in isolation.
